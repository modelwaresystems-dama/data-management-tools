01The idea in one minute

The calculator answers two questions a data leader is always asked at budget time:

  • What is the status quo costing us? An unstandardized team quietly loses money two ways — capacity burned on rework and reconciling undocumented silos, and the cost of recruiting and ramping replacements every time someone leaves. We call the sum of these the drain.
  • What would fixing it return? A two-engine programme — certifying a core group and operationalising the standards across the whole function — recovers a share of that lost capacity and keeps people longer. Measured against the one-off cost, that's the recovery, shown as payback (from go-live) and a 3-year NPV.
In plain English  Drain is what you lose by doing nothing. The upskilling case is what you get back by acting. The calculator shows the drain first because it's the more believable, more persuasive number — then the return.

04The results, part 1 — the drain

The cost of the status quo, shown as one headline figure and two components.

Capability & silo drain

In plain English  The slice of your total payroll classified Non-Value-Add (NVA) in the Know Your Value work-mix — capacity that delivers no Business Value-Add. It's the same bucket whether the hours go to rework and reconciling undocumented silos, or to personally-rewarding “passion projects” off the strategic roadmap: a passion project may grow the individual, but it adds no business value and is invisible to leadership. At 12% on a R10.8m payroll, that's R1.3m of paid time producing nothing the business asked for.
capability drain = NVA share d × headcount N × salary S

Talent-churn drain

In plain English  What it costs to keep replacing people who leave — recruitment plus the productivity lost while each replacement ramps up. This is the Employee Lifetime Value you keep re-buying instead of growing.
people leaving / yr = headcount N × turnover Tr
cost to replace one = (recruit% × S) + (ramp months × ramp output% × S/12)
churn drain = people leaving × cost to replace one

Total annual drain

total drain = capability drain + churn drain

Shown big at the top of the result, with a per-head figure (total drain ÷ headcount) underneath so you can quote it as "roughly R X per data professional, every year."

05The results, part 2 — the two-engine case

What it costs to act, and what acting returns. The case is a two-engine programme, and recovery is timed to a realistic rollout.

The two engines  Engine 1 — Certification (the catalyst): certify a core group on the framework; it gives them a shared language and is the lever that retains them. Engine 2 — Policy operationalisation & validation (the mechanism): build, embed and validate the standards across the whole function. Because Engine 2 lifts everyone, capability recovery scales with the full team N — not just the heads you certify. Churn saving scales with the certified share, since that's who the pathway retains.

Why payback is shown from go-live  The programme runs over an implementation runway (default 5 months) before steady-state recovery begins, so a headline "pays back in 2 months" measured from signing would be misleading. The tool reports the operational payback (from go-live) and the overall breakeven month, so the timeline survives CFO scrutiny.

The result page also draws two bars — the annual drain against the much smaller "invest vs recover" bar — and a deployment line (runway · full-recovery month · breakeven), to make the punchline visual: doing nothing is the expensive option. The three-year ROI multiple is still computed but deliberately not headlined — for a one-off cost against recurring recovery it runs into the hundreds of percent, which reads as hype; payback period and NPV are the defensible CFO framing.

06The results, part 3 — Employee Lifetime Value

The hook that opens the campaign — ELV — made concrete. Three figures.

Average tenure (ELV horizon)

How long the average person stays, before and after. A team losing 15% a year turns over completely in about 6.7 years; cut turnover by a pathway and that horizon stretches. Longer tenure means more value from each person you've already invested in.

tenure = 1 ÷ turnover rate   (e.g. 1 ÷ 0.15 = 6.7 yrs)
Team productivity uplift

The capacity standardizing gives back, as a percentage of total team output. Because Engine 2 lifts the whole function, this is not scaled down by how many you certify.

uplift = recover% × drag d
Employee Lifetime Value uplift

Tenure and productivity combined into a single ELV movement: people stay longer and produce more, so the lifetime value of each data professional rises.

ELV uplift = ( (new tenure ÷ old tenure) × (1 + productivity uplift) − 1 ) × 100

09Using the numbers well

  • Lead with the drain, not the ROI multiple. The drain (e.g. R1.9m a year) and the per-head figure are the believable, persuasive numbers in a budget meeting. They're hard to argue with because they come straight from your own headcount and salaries.
  • Treat a very high ROI with care. Because an annual recovery is being compared to a one-off cost, the 3-year ROI can run into the hundreds of percent. That's arithmetically correct, but a four-figure ROI reads as "vendor maths" to a sceptical CFO — which is why this tool leads with payback and the ramp-adjusted NPV instead. Quote the operational payback from go-live and the overall breakeven month, not a headline multiple.
  • Be honest about the timeline. Recovery doesn't start the day you sign — it begins after the implementation runway. The model already reflects this: payback is measured from go-live, and the NPV credits year one only for its post-go-live months. Present it that way and the numbers survive scrutiny.
  • Tune the assumptions to your reality before you present. Recruitment cost, ramp length, certification price and the Engine 2 cost are the most likely to differ from the defaults. Setting them to your real figures turns the model from a generic estimate into your own evidence.
  • It's a budgeting aid, not a forecast. The figures are indicative, designed to frame a conversation and size a decision — not to predict an exact P&L outcome.

10Glossary of terms

Plain-language definitions for the terms used in this guide. A in this tool tag marks terms specific to how this calculator models things; the rest are general data-management or finance terms.

BNVA — Business Non-Value-Adding

Necessary overhead — compliance, admin, reporting, meetings — that keeps things running but isn't itself the value the business asked for. One of the three categories in the Know Your Value work-mix; the strategy is to contain or automate it.

Business Value-Add — BVA

Work tied to one of the organisation's strategic data initiatives — value the business has explicitly asked for. The goal of standardizing is to convert lost capacity into BVA.

Capability & silo drain in this tool

The slice of your team's total pay classified Non-Value-Add — rework, untangling undocumented silos and off-roadmap passion projects — instead of going to Business Value-Add. Worked out as the NVA share times the total cost of the team.

CDMP — Certified Data Management Professional

The professional certification this upskilling is built around (Engine 1). Earning it signals that a data professional has demonstrated command of the discipline, measured against the DMBOK framework.

Churn

The ongoing cycle of people leaving and being replaced. The cost of that cycle — recruiting and ramping each replacement — is the talent-churn drain. Driven by the turnover rate.

DAMA — Data Management Association

The international professional body for data management, whose DMBOK framework underpins the certification and the standards referenced here.

Data silos

Pockets of data (and knowledge) held separately by individuals or teams, often undocumented, so others can't easily find, trust or reuse them. Reconciling silos is a major source of NVA effort.

Discount rate

The rate used to bring future money back to what it's worth today, for the NPV. A rand recovered next year is worth less than a rand today; the discount rate (12% by default) captures that.

DMBOK — Data Management Body of Knowledge

DAMA's reference framework setting out the knowledge areas of professional data management. Standardizing and certifying a team is measured against it.

Drag / NVA share in this tool

The percentage of a team's paid capacity that is Non-Value-Add — lost to a lack of standardization. A mostly ad-hoc, undocumented team carries a high share (around 20%); a mature, well-documented one carries low (around 5%). Set by your "team standardization" choice.

Drain in this tool

The total annual cost of leaving the team as it is — the capability & silo drain plus the talent-churn drain. In short, the cost of doing nothing.

Dual-engine programme in this tool

The structure of the case. Engine 1 — Certification (the catalyst): certify a core group, giving them a shared language and a reason to stay. Engine 2 — Policy operationalisation & validation (the mechanism): embed and validate the standards across the whole function. Capability recovery is attributed to Engine 2, so it scales with the full team, not only those certified.

Employee Lifetime Value — ELV

The total useful change a person creates over their tenure — for the business and the people around them — relative to what it costs to employ and grow them. It rises when people stay longer and produce more, and falls when you constantly re-buy value through new hires. The central idea behind this calculator.

Fully-loaded salary

The true annual cost of employing someone: base pay plus benefits, tax, tooling and overhead — not just take-home pay. Using the loaded figure keeps the drain a realistic cost rather than an understatement.

Growth Architect Console — part of Know Your Value

The manager-side companion to the KYV Workbook. A department lead (the "Growth Architect") imports the team's workbooks and the Console rolls them up into a department value picture — work-mix, VA-aligned %, initiative coverage and a stakeholder-feedback loop. It is the source of the work-mix telemetry the business case draws on.

Implementation runway in this tool

How long the programme rolls out before steady-state recovery begins (default 5 months): training plus parallel policy work, then operationalisation and QA. Payback and breakeven are timed from the end of this runway, not from signing.

Indicative model

A calculation built to size and frame a decision, not to predict an exact financial result. The figures are there to guide a budgeting conversation, not to forecast a precise profit-and-loss outcome.

Know Your Value — KYV

The value-management asset this case draws on. Each individual fills in the KYV Workbook — classifying their work-mix (VA / BNVA / NVA) against the company's strategic data initiatives — and the Growth Architect Console aggregates everyone into a department value picture. It supplies the evidence that a share of capacity is NVA rather than Business Value-Add.

Net Present Value — NPV

The recovery over three years brought back to today's money using the discount rate, minus the investment. Here it is ramp-adjusted: year one is credited only for the months of steady-state recovery that fall after the implementation runway, so the figure is deliberately conservative. The defensible CFO headline alongside payback.

Non-Value-Add — NVA

Work that delivers no Business Value-Add — rework, duplication, waste, and personally-rewarding passion projects off the strategic roadmap. From the business's standpoint these are the same bucket: capacity that should be recovered. The capability drain is the NVA share of payroll.

Operational payback in this tool

How many months of steady-state recovery it takes to cover the investment — measured from operational go-live (the end of the implementation runway), not from signing. Reported alongside the overall breakeven month so the timeline is honest.

Overall breakeven in this tool

The programme-lifecycle month at which the investment is fully repaid: the implementation runway plus the operational payback. For the worked example, around Month 11.

Passion project — a Win-Win Matrix quadrant

Work that is high in personal value but low in business value — "fun to do, but invisible to leadership." It may grow the individual, but it delivers no Business Value-Add, so in value terms it counts as NVA, not a separate category.

Policy operationalisation & validation — Engine 2

Building, embedding and validating the standards across the function. Operationalisation turns rules into automated data-quality checks, data-steward workflows and dashboards; validation is the continuous testing and auditing that keeps the standards live in production. The mechanism that actually recovers capability.

Productivity uplift

The extra useful output the team gains once standardizing recovers part of the NVA capacity, as a percentage of total team output. Because Engine 2 lifts the whole function, it isn't scaled down by how many you certify.

Ramp (ramp-up)

The settling-in period after someone joins before they reach full productivity — modelled here at about four months. During ramp they're useful but not yet at full value, so part of their salary is a cost without full return. (Distinct from the programme's implementation runway.)

Recruitment cost per hire

The cost of replacing one person: agency fees, advertising and the internal time spent hiring. Modelled as a percentage of salary (20% by default).

Return on Investment — ROI

The gain from an investment relative to its cost, as a percentage. The 3-year ROI is still computed but deliberately not headlined: comparing recurring recovery to a one-off cost produces a multiple in the hundreds of percent, which reads as hype. Payback and NPV are the figures to lead with.

Standardization

How consistent, documented and shared a team's ways of working are. Higher standardization means less NVA (lower drag), and it is what the two-engine programme is designed to build.

Talent-churn drain in this tool

The annual cost of replacing people who leave: recruitment plus the productivity lost while each replacement ramps up.

Tenure

The average length of time a person stays with the organisation. Modelled as 1 ÷ turnover rate, so a 15% turnover implies roughly a 6.7-year horizon. Longer tenure lifts Employee Lifetime Value.

Turnover (voluntary turnover)

The share of the team that chooses to leave each year. It is the main driver of churn cost; a clear, certified career pathway is modelled as reducing it.

VA / Value-Adding

Work that directly creates value the business wants. One of the three categories in the Know Your Value work-mix (VA / BNVA / NVA); the strategy is to expand it.

VA-aligned — Know Your Value metric

The share of hours that are both VA-classified and sit on a strategic data initiative — the Console's headline metric. The gap between VA and VA-aligned is value being created that the business hasn't (yet) asked for.
