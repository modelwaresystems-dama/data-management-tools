Phase 1 · Baseline

Measure where the organisation stands — without declaring a verdict

A live, two-dimensional Responsible-AI maturity assessment. The figures are an input to human judgment, never a pass/fail.

Two pillars — and why we assess both. The framework rests on two questions, asked separately because they fail separately.

Pillar A — Organizational maturity
“Is the organisation built to be responsible?”
The enabling environment — 13 sub-components of governance, risk processes, policy, semantics, leadership, AI literacy and value. You assign each a level 1–5.
Shows: that the organisation has the structures, accountability and capability to do responsible AI consistently and sustainably (the corporate governance code conformance, and the capacity for value).
Pillar B — Operational maturity
“Is responsibility actually present in the systems we run?”
The system-level safeguards on the live platform — 8 risk dimensions: privacy, fairness, transparency, accountability, human oversight, reliability, societal benefit and GenAI controls. Each measure is in place or not.
Shows: that the safeguards exist in practice, in the product affected stakeholders actually touches, today.
Why both are needed. Neither is sufficient alone. A strong organisation with weak operational controls is paper compliance — good intentions that never reach the system. Strong operational controls without the organisation are fragile and unaccountable — they depend on individuals and erode without governance, funding and oversight. Pillar A makes responsibility durable and accountable; Pillar B makes it real where it counts. Examining where the two diverge is what makes the result defensible.

The five levels — how you rate each organisational sub-component — score 0/25/50/75/100 on an evidence-of-achievement test:

1 · InitialAbsent / ad-hoc; no records.
2 · AssessingDefined or being assessed (draft, plan); not yet routine.
3 · DeterminedOperationalised & performed consistently by capable people.
4 · ManagedEffectiveness & efficiency measured (KPIs/metrics, reviewed).
5 · OptimizedImprovement goals set & pursued.

How the levels apply to Operational. You don’t pick a 1–5 level per operational measure — each is binary (in place or not). The dimension’s percentage is instead mapped onto these same band names (Initial → Optimized) on the Scorecard and Report. So the five levels label both dimensions: you set them directly on the Organisational side, while the Operational level is computed from how many applicable measures are fully operationalised.

Named Evidence + justification. The Evidence tab is the register: one named Evidence item per level and measure. Each Evidence is a collection of Artefacts — the checklist of contents you must hold — mapped to the corporate governance code principles and RAI standards (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act, data-protection law, FAIR/responsible-data). Cite the Evidence ID and confirm you hold all its Artefacts.

Report. The Report tab compiles the full results — scores, level profile with cited Evidence, Phase 2 priorities and the human sign-off — and can be printed, saved as PDF, or downloaded as a standalone HTML file.

Import / export. Export .xlsx saves a five-tab workbook (round-trips via Import). Nothing is stored online — the file is your record.

Two board-level sub-components were added in response to the the corporate governance code / RAI review (flagged Governance/RAI review): Board & Executive AI Literacy, and Performance & Value Creation. All other sub-components — including Policy & Standards, Semantic Governance and the GenAI dimension — are integral, supporting the corporate governance code and RAI obligations directly.

AI & Data Harms Taxonomy (generic)

Seven branches to adapt per organisation — the benefits to weigh, the harms to surface, the stakeholders affected, and where mitigation sits.

Affected Stakeholders

  • Data subjects / customers
  • Vulnerable or protected groups
  • Wider society
  • The organisation itself

Intended Benefits

  • Better, faster decisions
  • Improved products & services
  • New insight & value
  • Fairer access or outcomes

Privacy & Data Protection

  • Excessive collection / minimisation failure
  • Re-identification of individuals
  • Cross-border / third-party leakage
  • Weak or unclear consent

Fairness & Bias

  • Biased training data
  • Discriminatory outcomes
  • Under-representation / exclusion
  • Proxy discrimination

Technical & Model Harms

  • Inaccuracy / unreliability
  • GenAI hallucination
  • Gaming / data poisoning
  • Model drift

Transparency & Accountability

  • Opaque decisions
  • No recourse or appeal
  • Undisclosed AI use
  • Unclear ownership

Governance & Mitigation

  • Automation / anchoring bias in review
  • AI Impact & Design Checklist
  • Ethics / Governance Review Board
  • Independent assurance

The One-Page AI Impact & Design Checklist

Showing the blank prompts. Switch to Worked example to see them answered for an illustrative project.

1 · Purpose, Benefit & Use Cases
Integrates: Intended Benefits · ethical & societal considerations · AI-function risk
Value & benefit — What specific benefit does this deliver, to whom, and does it return value to the people whose data is used — or only to the organisation?
Faster, more consistent eligibility decisions for applicants and lower processing cost. The team commits to an applicant-facing benefit — a quicker decision and a plain-language explanation — so value is returned to data subjects, not captured only internally.
AI function & technical risk — If a model or LLM produces the output, what is the risk of inaccurate or hallucinated results, and how is every output checked?
An LLM drafts the explanation text for each decision. Hallucination (invented reasons) is a real risk, so explanations are generated only from the model’s actual decision factors and validated against them — no free-text invention.
Automation / anchoring bias — Could reviewers rubber-stamp the system’s output instead of forming an independent judgement first?
Reviewers assess a sample independently before seeing the model’s recommendation, to avoid rubber-stamping.

2 · Stakeholder Impact & Fairness
Integrates: Affected Stakeholders · Fairness & Bias · Technical & Model Harms
Stakeholder mapping — Who exactly is impacted — direct users, vulnerable or protected groups who may be excluded, those declined, front-line staff, and the organisation itself?
Applicants; protected groups (age, gender, disability, and proxies such as postcode); declined applicants; front-line staff; the organisation.
Bias & exclusion — Could specific groups be under-served or treated unfairly, and could proxies reproduce protected-attribute discrimination?
The model may under-serve thin-file or protected groups, and postcode could act as a proxy for a protected attribute. ⚑ discrimination risk → register
Reliability, gaming & drift — Could inputs be gamed, could data be stale, and will performance drift after launch?
Applicants could game self-reported fields; data can go stale. Verification of key fields and post-launch drift monitoring are required. ⚑ gaming / drift → register
Team response & decision: …
3 · Consent, Data Minimisation & Privacy
Integrates: Privacy & Data Protection
Data minimisation — Are we collecting only the data the stated purpose actually needs?
Collect only the fields the decision needs; nice-to-have personal data is dropped from the pipeline.
Re-identification & sharing — Is personal data adequately minimised, pseudonymised or aggregated before analytics or sharing, so individuals cannot be re-identified?
Analytics run on pseudonymised data; raw personal data is not shared with third parties.
Consent & the right to human review — Is notice given in plain language, and are people told an automated decision is being made and that they can request human review?
A plain-language notice explains the automated decision and the right to request a human review, in line with applicable data-protection law.
GenAI & cross-border leaks — If data is processed through a third-party or cross-border LLM, what prevents confidentiality breaches or unlawful transfers?
The LLM receives only de-identified decision factors, never raw personal data. Processor location and cross-border terms are still to be confirmed. ⚑ open action → DPO
Team response & decision: …
4 · Accountability & Actionable Mitigation
Integrates: Transparency & Accountability · Governance harms · Outcome trade-offs
Trade-off evaluation — Do the intended benefits outweigh the identified risks for this specific use — and who made that call?
Benefit (speed, consistency) is judged to outweigh the risks — but only once fairness testing and a human-review route are in place.
Accountability & escalation — Does this involve high-risk data or automated decisions with legal or material effect that require escalation to the Ethical Governance Board?
Automated decisions with a material effect on applicants are high-risk, so the project is escalated to the Ethical Governance Board.
Actionable mitigation — today — What specific engineering or design changes are required now, and who owns each?
  1. Fairness testing across protected attributes and proxies before launch. [Data Science]
  2. Human-review and appeal route for declined applicants. [Product]
  3. Explanations grounded in real decision factors — no LLM invention. [ML lead]
  4. Confirm the LLM processor terms and any cross-border transfer safeguards. [DPO]
  5. Post-launch drift and override monitoring. [Operations]

The operational rule. Formatting the taxonomy as challenging questions — “Could a proxy reproduce discrimination?”, “Does this return value to the people whose data is used?” — turns a static risk list into an active governance tool that a review board can debate while the design is still easy to change.
